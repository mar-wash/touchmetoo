The Project

Touch Me, Too! is an interactive digital artwork and sensory installation that explores the threshold between language, technology, and embodiment.

Drawing from conceptual metaphor theory, the project begins with a linguistic observation: we routinely describe computers using the language of the human body. Machines have “memory,” “vision,” “voice,” “nerves,” “brains,” and “bodies.”

If metaphor structures thought, what does it mean that we so readily understand COMPUTERS as HUMANS?

The installation asks: What happens to our experience of technology when the linguistic boundary between humans and computers begins to blur?

The Artist

Born 2003, Houston, TX.
Based in Boulder, CO.

Marshall Washington is an interdisciplinary artist and researcher whose work explores the threshold between language, technology, and embodiment. After receiving dual bachelor's degrees in Linguistics and Chinese from the University of Oklahoma, Marshall is a current master's student within the Linguistics department at the University of Colorado Boulder. As a linguist, he seeks to understand what happens when humans share language with computers. Across scholarly and creative work, he returns to one central question: what happens when language breaks its own rules? His projects incorporate linguistics, anthropology, digital humanities, and creative computing.
