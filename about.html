Touch Me, Too!

Touch Me, Too! is an interactive digital artwork and sensory installation that explores the threshold between language, technology, and embodiment.

Drawing from conceptual metaphor theory, the project begins with a linguistic observation: we routinely describe computers using the language of the human body. Machines have “memory,” “vision,” “voice,” “nerves,” “brains,” and “bodies.” If metaphor structures thought, as Lakoff (1992) argues, what does it mean that we so readily understand COMPUTERS as HUMANS?

Through a corpus analysis of English-language data, the project traces explicit mappings between human anatomy and computer architecture, identifying where body parts are projected onto hardware and software systems. Where does the body end and the machine begin?

In its physical form, Touch Me, Too! becomes a sensory environment in which visitors are invited to encounter the computer as something that can touch and be touched. Participants may press, stroke, or hold components that respond through vibration, heat, or sound, collapsing the emotional distance between user and interface.

The installation asks: What happens to our experience of technology when the linguistic boundary between humans and computers begins to blur?

Artist Bio

Born 2003, Houston, TX. Based in Boulder, CO.

Marshall Washington is an interdisciplinary artist and researcher whose work explores the threshold between language, technology, and embodiment. After receiving dual bachelor's degrees in Linguistics and Chinese from the University of Oklahoma, he is currently a master’s student in the Linguistics department at the University of Colorado Boulder.

As a linguist, he seeks to understand what happens when humans share language with computers. Across both scholarly and creative work, he returns to one central question: what happens when language breaks its own rules?
